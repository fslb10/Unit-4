Part One · Introduction

Scripture as Inspired Word

Catholics believe the Bible is inspired by God, but inspiration does not mean the human authors became robots.

The Bible is at the heart of Catholic faith because it reveals God, His plan, and the saving truth fulfilled in Jesus Christ. Sacred Scripture has many human authors, but one divine author: God.

Divine inspiration means the Holy Spirit guided the biblical authors so that they wrote what God wanted expressed for our salvation. God worked through their language, culture, abilities, personality, and historical setting.

This is why Catholics read Scripture with reverence and intelligence. We take seriously that Scripture is God's Word, and we also take seriously the human context through which that Word was written.

"God is the author of Sacred Scripture because he inspired its human authors."Catechism of the Catholic Church, §105

Part Three · Inspiration and Inerrancy

God's Word in Human Words

The Catholic approach holds together two truths: Scripture is divine, and Scripture is genuinely human.

The Bible was written over many centuries by many human authors. Yet Catholics believe God guided these authors through the Holy Spirit. Their writing is not merely human opinion. It is inspired Scripture.

Because God is the principal author, Scripture teaches without error the truth God wanted revealed for salvation. This is called inerrancy. It does not mean the Bible is trying to answer every modern scientific or historical question in the way a textbook would.

Instead, inerrancy means Scripture reliably teaches saving truth: who God is, who we are, what sin does, how God saves, and how we are called to live in faith and holiness.

The Bible is not less sacred because humans wrote it. God chose to speak through human words.

Part Four · How Scripture Formed

Oral, Written, and Edited Tradition

Scripture did not appear all at once. God's revelation unfolded through history and was preserved by a faith community.

The Old Testament tells the story of God forming a covenant people: Abraham, Moses, the Law, the prophets, the exile, and the hope for a Messiah. These experiences were remembered, proclaimed, written down, and collected.

This process can be described in three stages. First, stories and teachings were passed down orally. Then they were written. Later, the community collected and refined these writings so they could be handed on faithfully.

The New Testament centers on Jesus Christ and the early Church. It includes the four Gospels, Acts of the Apostles, letters to Christian communities, and Revelation. The New Testament is shorter than the Old, but it contains the definitive revelation of God in Jesus.

Part Five · The Four Gospels

One Good News, Four Portraits

Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John proclaim the same Jesus, but each does so from a unique perspective.

Matthew

Matthew writes especially for a Jewish-Christian audience and emphasizes Jesus as the Messiah who fulfills the Law and prophets.

Mark

Mark writes in a fast-moving style, stressing Jesus' power, suffering, and the cost of discipleship.

Luke

Luke writes an orderly account for Gentile converts, highlighting Jesus as Savior of all, especially the poor, sinners, and outsiders.

John

John uses poetic and symbolic language to reveal Jesus' divine identity as the Word made flesh and Son of God.

The Gospels sometimes differ in detail, order, or emphasis, but they converge in faithfully proclaiming Jesus' life, death, and resurrection.

Part Six · Interpretation

Exegesis, Not Eisegesis

Faithful interpretation means drawing meaning from Scripture, not forcing our own agenda into it.

Hermeneutics is the study of interpretation. For Catholics, it means reading Scripture with attention to both the literal meaning and the deeper spiritual message God communicates through the Church.

Exegesis asks careful questions: Who wrote this? Who was the original audience? What genre is it? What was happening historically and culturally? What did the sacred author intend?

Eisegesis does the opposite. It reads personal bias into the text. This is dangerous because it can make the Bible seem to support ideas the text never actually meant. For example, people have misused Scripture to defend slavery, revenge, racism, or personal prejudice by ignoring context.

A faithful reader asks, "What is God saying through this text?" not "How can I use this text to support what I already think?"

Part Seven · A Catholic Method

Five Steps for Reading Scripture

A simple method helps readers move from the text's original meaning to personal application without skipping context.

1. Read the passage

Understand the literal story or teaching. What happens? Who is involved? What is said?

2. Study the background

Look at history, culture, location, audience, and genre. The original setting matters.

3. Notice key words and images

Pay attention to repeated words, symbolic images, actions, and contrasts.

4. Discover the message

Ask what spiritual truth God is communicating through the passage.

5. Apply it to life

Ask how this Word calls you to faith, repentance, trust, mercy, or action today.

This method can be applied to the Prodigal Son, Jesus calming the storm, and the Widow's Offering. In each case, context deepens the message and helps us avoid shallow interpretation.

Part Eight · Catholic Tradition and Culture

Canon, Tradition, and the Church

The Bible was not separated from the Church. The Church discerned, preserved, interpreted, and handed on the inspired books.

The canon of Scripture is the official list of inspired books. Catholics recognize 73 books in the Bible. This canon was discerned through Sacred Tradition and the authority of the Church.

Early councils, guided by the Holy Spirit, confirmed which books belonged in the Bible. The Council of Rome in AD 382 and later the Council of Trent in AD 1546 are important moments in this process.

The Magisterium, the Church's teaching authority, guides interpretation so Scripture is read in continuity with the apostolic faith. Scripture and Tradition work together in transmitting God's Revelation.

St. Jerome translated the Scriptures into Latin around AD 400, producing the Vulgate. His famous saying, "Ignorance of Scripture is ignorance of Christ," reminds Catholics that Scripture study is not optional decoration. It leads us to Jesus.
